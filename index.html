Abstract

Vision‚ÄìLanguage‚ÄìAction (VLA) systems have advanced semantic reasoning for robots, yet achieving robust whole-body humanoid control that couples semantic understanding with dynamic adaptability remains a central challenge. Existing approaches often lack explicit modeling of high-dimensional dynamics and rely on training data that is fragmented and non-standardized, limiting generalization across domains and reliable recovery under perturbations.

In this work, we present MIRAGE, a unified mobile manipulation framework for robust humanoid control, which seamlessly integrates standardized data generation with robust execution policies. A teacher expert trained with reinforcement learning under extensive domain randomization generates large volumes of standardized trajectories, mitigating the long-standing data scarcity in embodied intelligence. Our framework introduces three key innovations: SCHEMA (Sparse Command Handling for Embodied Motion Abstraction), MIRROR (Memory-Integrated Robust Representation for Online Robustness), and RISE (Recovery via Inferred State Endpoints), which together enable generalizable training signals, robust control under domain shifts, and long-horizon recovery behaviors.

Extensive experiments show that MIRAGE consistently converts SCHEMA-formatted inputs into torque-level humanoid control and achieves strong robustness across diverse domain shifts. Quantitatively, MIRAGE reduces trajectory error by ‚àº17%, raises strict success by ‚àº16 percentage points, improves push-recovery by ‚àº34 points, and attains 61% fall-recovery where baselines fail. Our work advances the feasibility of resilient humanoid deployment in unstructured environments.

MIRAGE Framework

MIRAGE pipeline integrates teacher-student training, standardized data generation, and robust policy execution for whole-body humanoid control.

Three Core Innovations

SCHEMA

Sparse Command Handling for Embodied Motion Abstraction

Unified sparse-joint protocol based on SMPL-X key body joints, enabling generalizable training signals from language tokens or sparse keyframes.

MIRROR

Memory-Integrated Robust Representation for Online Robustness

Leverages recent action-state histories as implicit memory to align latent dynamics, stabilizing control under domain shifts.

RISE

Recovery via Inferred State Endpoints

Distills diverse expert recoveries into shared terminal representations, guiding long-horizon behaviors like fall-and-get-up.

Ablation Study: MaskedMimic Pro

What is MaskedMimic Pro?

MaskedMimic Pro is our ablation baseline designed to isolate the contribution of MIRAGE's key innovations. Built upon the original MaskedMimic method and optimized for the Unitree G1 humanoid robot, it features:

  • Fine-tuned Training Parameters: Carefully adjusted hyperparameters tailored for the G1's specific kinematics and dynamics
  • Enhanced Transformer Architecture: Expanded the transformer encoder from 4 layers to 6 layers, increasing model capacity and representation power
  • Robot-Specific Optimization: Design choices specifically targeting the Unitree G1's unique characteristics and constraints

Unitree G1

Optimized Target Platform

Ablation Design

MaskedMimic Pro serves as our controlled ablation baseline, representing MIRAGE without the MIRROR component (Memory-Integrated Robust Representation for Online Robustness). This design allows us to isolate and quantify the contribution of MIRROR's implicit memory mechanism to overall system robustness and domain transfer performance.

Expert Training Process

Training Environment Setup

Our expert model training follows a large-scale parallel reinforcement learning approach. We utilize the terrain configuration from the original MaskedMimic paper (shown in the terrain diversity visualization below), which provides a comprehensive set of challenging surfaces including slopes, stairs, and uneven terrain.

Parallel Training Configuration

We deploy 4,096 robots evenly distributed across the training terrain. The physics simulation runs at 200 Hz, while the policy control operates at 50 Hz. Training continues for a total of 1 billion (1,000 million) control steps, which serves as our termination condition.

Reinforcement Learning Configuration

Each robot executes motion capture sequences from our training dataset with weighted sampling, collecting real-time state information and comparing it against ground truth trajectories to compute reward signals for reinforcement learning optimization.

Training Schedule

  • Collection Interval: 32 control steps per training update
  • Mini-batch Size: 16,384 samples per update
  • Training Epochs per Update: 4,096 robots √ó 32 steps √∑ 16,384 = 8 internal updates
  • Termination Condition: 1,000 million control steps at 50 Hz control frequency

Note: This expert training configuration is identical to the mechanism described in the MaskedMimic paper, ensuring fair comparison and reproducibility. The large-scale parallel training with 4,096 robots enables efficient exploration and robust policy learning across diverse terrain conditions and motion patterns.

Domain Randomization Strategy

Bridging the Sim-to-Real Gap

Our Domain Randomization (DR) strategy is carefully designed to balance model performance with diverse domain coverage, ensuring robust sim-to-sim and potential sim-to-real transfer. The approach introduces controlled variations across multiple aspects of the simulation environment:

Observation Randomization

Sensor Noise

Slight observation noise is added to sensor readings, simulating real-world measurement uncertainties while maintaining model accuracy.

Physical Parameter Randomization

  • Body Mass: Randomized within a specified range
  • Joint Stiffness: Varied to simulate different actuator characteristics
  • Friction: Contact friction coefficients randomized
  • Damping: Joint damping parameters varied

Actuator Latency Simulation

To mimic real-world hardware delays, joint command signals are probabilistically delayed by 1-2 control steps, simulating:

  • Communication latency
  • Actuator response time
  • Controller processing delays

External Perturbations

Random external forces are applied to the robot at random time intervals, simulating:

  • Physical contacts and collisions
  • Environmental disturbances
  • Unexpected external interactions

Design Philosophy

Our DR configuration strikes a careful balance between two competing objectives: (1) maintaining high task performance in the source domain, and (2) maximizing exposure to diverse input distributions that cover potential target domains. This balanced approach enables MIRAGE to achieve robust generalization across different simulators and physical conditions, as demonstrated in our sim-to-sim transfer experiments.

Training Pipeline

Stage 1: Teacher Training

Reinforcement learning with extensive domain randomization generates diverse, standardized trajectories that mitigate data scarcity in embodied AI.

  • Domain randomization
  • Expert policy learning

Stage 2: Student Training

Student policy learns from teacher demonstrations through imitation learning, incorporating MIRROR and RISE for enhanced robustness and recovery.

RISE üîÆ Future Work

Conceptual Design

Recovery via Inferred State Endpoints: Inspired by insights from the MaskedMimic codebase, we hypothesize a future capability where the student model receives future target states (sparse keyframes + temporal offset) as input during distillation. We conjecture this will enable the policy to infer optimal actions that maximize the probability of reaching target states under physical constraints, achieving higher-dimensional recovery mechanisms such as autonomous fall-and-get-up behaviors.

RISE: Vision for Advanced Recovery

Core Hypothesis: The expert model has learned extensive recovery behaviors (e.g., fall-and-get-up) from the training dataset. During online distillation, by modifying the input to include future target states (instead of current states), the student model may develop an emergent ability to reason about action sequences that lead to desired outcomes. This input modification transforms the distillation process from "imitate current actions" to "infer actions toward future goals," potentially enabling:

  • Inverse dynamics reasoning: Computing "what actions now" lead to "target state later"
  • Physics-aware planning: Respecting physical constraints in action inference
  • Complex recovery behaviors: Multi-step recovery like fall detection ‚Üí reorientation ‚Üí stand-up

This represents a promising direction for endowing embodied agents with higher-level recovery intelligence.

Sim-to-Sim Transfer Performance Comparison

Direct comparison of sim-to-sim transfer performance: IsaacLab ‚Üí Genesis simulator

MaskedMimic Pro
(No DR)

Poor Transfer: Trained in IsaacLab without DR, then transferred to Genesis. The policy overfits to the source simulator's specific dynamics and completely fails when encountering Genesis's different physics engine, demonstrating catastrophic sim-to-sim transfer failure.

MaskedMimic Pro
(With DR)

Moderate Transfer: Trained in IsaacLab with DR, then transferred to Genesis. DR prevents complete failure by exposing the model to varied dynamics. However, without historical memory, it learns a unified policy averaging across domains, unable to adapt to Genesis-specific characteristics for optimal performance.

MIRAGE
(With DR)

Superior Transfer: Trained in IsaacLab with DR, then transferred to Genesis. MIRROR's historical memory enables domain-aware adaptation: the policy senses Genesis-specific dynamics through action-state history and switches to the optimal strategy, achieving robust and natural locomotion.

Why Does MIRROR Make the Difference?

We hypothesize that historical memory is essential for handling DR environments. MIRROR's action-state history provides the robot with "proprioceptive intuition" ‚Äî the ability to sense its own historical states and actions. Without this memory, models trained under DR learn a unified policy that averages across all domains but cannot perceive domain-specific characteristics. While this prevents complete failure (as seen in MaskedMimic Pro with DR), it sacrifices optimal performance by lacking the ability to identify the current domain and adapt its strategy accordingly. In contrast, MIRROR enables domain-aware adaptation, allowing the policy to switch to the optimal strategy for each specific domain.
